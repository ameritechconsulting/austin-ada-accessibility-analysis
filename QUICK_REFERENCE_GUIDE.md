## PART 2: 20-MINUTE PRESENTATION TALK TRACK

### Opening (1 minute)
"Good [morning/afternoon]. Thank you for your time today. I'm presenting our comprehensive assessment of pedestrian crosswalk accessibility across Austin—a critical infrastructure issue affecting thousands of our residents.

We surveyed 175,609 crosswalks and found that while some neighborhoods have excellent accessibility, others face significant gaps that disproportionately impact our elderly residents, people with disabilities, and students. Today I'll share what we found and our plan to address it."

### Key Findings (3 minutes)
"Let me cut to the chase with three critical findings:

**Finding One: The Gap**  
Only 21.5% of Austin's crosswalks meet current ADA standards. That means 78.5% of our pedestrian infrastructure has accessibility issues. The most common problems are missing curb ramps, non-functional signals, and deteriorated surfaces that create trip hazards.

**Finding Two: The Equity Crisis**  
This isn't evenly distributed. Neighborhoods with lower incomes have significantly lower compliance rates. Our analysis shows a 17.6-point compliance gap between the highest and lowest-income areas. That's not acceptable.

**Finding Three: The Population Impact**  
This affects real people. Approximately 133,532 elderly residents have mobility limitations affected by these barriers. 5,130 students walk through non-compliant school zones daily. It's a safety and equity issue."

### Data Overview (2 minutes)
"Here's what our data shows:

We assessed 175,609 crosswalks across 10 neighborhoods. We evaluated curb ramps, pedestrian signals, surface conditions, and overall accessibility features. We also analyzed impact on vulnerable populations—elderly people, disabled residents, and students.

The patterns are clear: The downtown and wealthier neighborhoods have 30.8% compliance, while lower-income residential areas average just 13.2%. This gap is unacceptable and contradicts Austin's values of inclusion and equity."

### ADA Compliance & Curb Ramps (3 minutes)
"Let me focus on curb ramps because they're the foundation of accessibility. A curb ramp is literally the difference between someone being able to cross the street safely and being completely blocked.

**The ADA Standards are clear:**
- Every crosswalk needs a curb ramp
- It must have the right slope (1:12 or less)
- It must be at least 36 inches wide
- It must have tactile warning strips (the yellow bumpy surface)

**What we found:**
- 16.7% of crosswalks are missing ramps entirely
- 23.3% have improper slopes that cause wheelchairs to tip
- 64.4% lack tactile warnings, making them impossible for blind pedestrians to detect

This is a solvable problem. We know exactly what to do and how much it costs."

### Functional Condition Status (2 minutes)
"Beyond compliance, we assessed the physical condition of the infrastructure:

- **Excellent (A)**: 21.5% - Recently maintained, no issues
- **Good (B)**: 21.2% - Functional but showing normal wear
- **Fair (C)**: 29.4% - Visible deterioration, maintenance needed
- **Poor (D)**: 23.1% - Significant damage, repairs needed
- **Failed (F)**: 4.8% - Unsafe, immediate replacement required

The good news: Most of these are preventable through regular maintenance. The challenge: We're currently operating in crisis mode, fixing emergencies instead of preventing problems. That's more expensive and less effective."

### ADA Monitoring & Critical Insights (3 minutes)
"Here's what makes this winnable:

**One: We have a clear compliance target.** The Americans with Disabilities Act 2010 Standards tell us exactly what's required. There's no ambiguity.

**Two: We know the costs.** Our analysis shows the average curb ramp costs 1,007K, a signal costs 4K. We can calculate the full remediation budget: 506.3M over 5 years.

**Three: Prevention is cheaper than replacement.** Maintaining a crosswalk costs 3.0K annually. Replacing one after failure costs 25.0K. That's 67% more expensive. If we shift to preventive maintenance, we'll save money while improving safety.

**Four: We can target high-impact areas first.** We've identified 151 critical school crosswalks, 3958 transit hub connections, and 128 healthcare facility accesses. These serve the most vulnerable populations and serve the most people. We can achieve 100% compliance at these locations in 3 months, then expand.

**Five: Federal funding exists.** There are grants specifically for ADA compliance—USDOT grants, Americans with Disabilities Act funding, and state matching programs."

### Policy Recommendations (2 minutes)
"We're recommending a three-phase approach:

**Phase 1 - Critical (0-3 months): $506.3M**  
School crosswalks, transit hubs, healthcare facilities. These serve our most vulnerable populations. Target 100% compliance.

**Phase 2 - High-Traffic (3-12 months): $506.3M**  
Downtown, commercial areas, and neighborhoods with highest deficiency rates. Target 95%+ compliance.

**Phase 3 - Full System (1-2 years): $506.3M**  
Complete remaining improvements while transitioning to a preventive maintenance program that costs less than current emergency repairs.

This isn't just about legal compliance. It's about safety, equity, and inclusion. It's about making sure every Austin resident—elderly, disabled, student, or otherwise—can safely cross the street."

### Call to Action (1 minute)
"We're asking City Council to:

1. **Approve the remediation plan** - giving us authority to proceed
2. **Allocate $506.3M for Year 1** - funding Phase 1 critical locations
3. **Establish governance** - creating an ADA Compliance Committee
4. **Commit to transparency** - monthly dashboards and quarterly reports

This is doable, affordable, and aligned with our values as a city.

We have 133,532 elderly residents who will benefit. We have 5,130 students whose daily walk to school will be safer. We have 172,733 disabled residents who will gain independence. 

Austin can be the model for accessibility. Let's make it happen.

Do you have questions?"

## PART 7: FREQUENTLY ASKED QUESTIONS

**Q: Why now? Why is this so urgent?**  
A: Because 172,733 residents with disabilities are currently blocked from safe pedestrian access. Because 5,130 students walk through hazardous school zones. Because we're spending emergency repair money instead of prevention. Because the law requires it. The time to act is now.

**Q: How much will this really cost?**  
A: Total remediation is approximately $506.3M over 5 years. That's about $393 per Austin resident spread over 5 years—about $79 per person annually. Compare that to the cost of a lawsuit (much higher) or the public health cost of reduced walking (significant). Prevention costs 1/3 the price of emergency replacement.

**Q: Where will the money come from?**  
A: Multiple sources: municipal budget ($506.3M), federal USDOT/ADA grants ($506.3M), state matching funds ($50.6M), development contributions ($50.6M). We're not waiting for perfect funding—we're using a phased approach so early phases fund early and secure momentum for later funding.

**Q: How long will the construction take?**  
A: Phase 1 (critical locations) takes 3 months. Phase 2 (high-traffic areas) takes another 9 months. Phase 3 (full system) spreads over 1-2 years. We're done with most major disruption in 12 months. Then it's preventive maintenance.

**Q: Will construction disrupt traffic and businesses?**  
A: Yes, some disruption is unavoidable, but we're mitigating it. We're scheduling evening/weekend work in downtown to minimize business impact. We're doing school work during summer when possible. We're coordinating with transit to work around schedules. We're not trying to hide disruption; we're managing it responsibly.

**Q: What about snow and winter? Won't that delay things?**  
A: That's why we're starting now (spring) and planning work seasonally. Summer is ideal for asphalt and concrete work. Spring/fall for design and curb work. Winter for planning and procurement. We're building delays into the timeline. It's doable.

**Q: How do we know this will actually get done?**  
A: Monthly dashboard reporting (public), quarterly Council briefings, Task Force oversight, accountability for contractors. We're creating visibility, not hiding progress. If we slip, everyone will know immediately. That's the whole point.

**Q: What if more problems are found during construction?**  
A: We've assessed 100% of the system thoroughly. When we do the actual work, some additional issues may emerge. We've budgeted 10% contingency for unexpected conditions. If scope grows, we adjust timelines and budget accordingly—but the priority framework stays the same.

**Q: Why focus on schools, transit, and healthcare first?**  
A: Because that's where the most vulnerable people are, and those crossings serve the most people. A school crosswalk serves 151 students daily. A transit stop serves thousands. A hospital serves patients with medical emergencies. That's basic triage—help the most people, serve the most vulnerable first.

**Q: What's the equity angle? Why is this about equity?**  
A: Because 13.2% compliance in low-income neighborhoods vs. 30.8% in high-income neighborhoods is unjust. Accessibility shouldn't depend on your zip code. Our recommendation framework explicitly prioritizes equity—underserved communities get resources first. This is both a legal mandate and a moral one.

**Q: Who's going to maintain this after we fix it?**  
A: That's the whole point. We're transitioning from emergency-response to preventive maintenance. We're hiring an ADA Coordinator. We're deploying an asset management system. We're budgeting for regular maintenance. This isn't a one-time project; it's a culture change in how we steward our infrastructure.

**Q: Can I report problems with crosswalks?**  
A: Yes. We're establishing an accessibility hotline and online reporting system. Call [Number] or visit [Website]. We'll assess within 48 hours. If it's a safety hazard, we'll fix it within 30 days. You're part of the solution.
